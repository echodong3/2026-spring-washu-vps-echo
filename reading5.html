One idea that stood out to me in The Coming Age of Calm Technology is the concept of designing technology to move between the center and periphery of attention. The authors argue that future technologies should not constantly demand our focus, but instead exist in the background and only come to the center when necessary. This idea feels especially relevant today, where notifications, apps, and constant connectivity often overwhelm users rather than support them.

I found the example of the “dangling string” particularly compelling. Unlike traditional dashboards or screens that require active attention, the string communicates network activity through subtle motion and sound, allowing users to remain aware without being distracted. This demonstrates how information can be conveyed effectively without overloading the user, which challenges the common assumption that more detailed or explicit interfaces are always better.

The reading also made me reflect on how most modern technologies fail to be “calm.” Social media platforms, for instance, are intentionally designed to capture and hold attention at the center, often leading to stress and distraction. In contrast, the authors’ vision of ubiquitous computing suggests a future where technology becomes more like electricity—essential but invisible. This shift would require designers to prioritize human well-being and cognitive balance over level of engagement.
