Sacasas’s argument that artifacts already have ethics, rather than needing morality to be “added” later, was the most interesting part of the reading. I thought his point pushes beyond the common idea that technology is neutral and only becomes good or bad depending on how people use it. Instead, he argues that technology shapes our habits, feelings, relationships, and even our sense of time and responsibility. That makes ethics much deeper than just asking whether a tool is used correctly. The long lists of questions at the end of the reading is intriguing to me, especially the ones asking what kind of person a technology makes us, what it encourages us to notice or ignore, and what responsibilities it might outsource. Those questions made me think about how often we judge technology by convenience or efficiency without asking what it changes about everyday life. For example, a phone or social media app is not just something we “use”; it also trains attention, affects relationships, and changes how we spend time. I also found his question, “What would the world be like if everyone used this technology exactly as I use it?” especially powerful because it shifts ethics from a private choice to a social one. It suggests that even ordinary personal uses of technology can have wider consequences. Overall, this reading made me think that ethical questions about technology should not only focus on extreme harms, but also on the quieter ways tools shape people and society over time.
