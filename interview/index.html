Subject — Chloe Liu2026

Chloe Liu is a freshman exploring film and media at WashU. Having grown up between countries, she approaches storytelling through a deeply personal and cross-cultural lens. Her work often reflects on movement, memory, and the shifting meaning of home.

The Conversation

Recorded 2026 · Lightly edited for clarity and length · Interview conducted in person
Scene 01 — Introduction
Q

Can you introduce yourself and tell us a bit about what you study?

CL

Sure! I'm Chloe, I'm a junior studying Film and Media. I came in thinking I wanted to be a screenwriter, but I've slowly migrated toward directing — realizing that the script is just the beginning of a much longer conversation.

Scene 02 — Origin
Q

What drew you to film in the first place?

CL

Honestly? A specific scene in a film I watched when I was about twelve — I won't say which one, but it made me cry without fully understanding why. That gap, between the feeling and the explanation — that's what I wanted to explore. Film operates in that space better than almost any other medium.

Scene 03 — Academic Formation
Q

How has your academic experience shaped your approach to filmmaking?

CL

It's given me a critical vocabulary I didn't have before. Understanding the male gaze, understanding how editing constructs meaning, how sound design is never neutral — these concepts rewired how I watch films. And once you can't unsee something, it changes how you make things too.

"Once you can't unsee something, it changes how you make things too."
Scene 04 — Representation
Q

You mentioned representation. Is that a theme in your own work?

CL

Absolutely. Growing up, I noticed the stories that were missing. Not just Asian American stories, but a certain kind of quiet, interior, unglamorous story. I want to make films that ask questions, not hand you the answers. My short film last semester was about a woman deciding whether or not to call her estranged mother — it was twelve minutes long and nothing "happened," but I was more proud of it than anything else I've made.

Scene 05 — Influences
Q

What filmmakers are you most inspired by?

CL

Agnès Varda, always. She made you feel like the camera was her hand reaching out to touch something. Chloé Zhao for how she blends documentary and fiction. And Wong Kar-wai — the way memory works in his films, as something slippery and beautiful and painful all at once. I watch In the Mood for Love at least once a semester. Every time it teaches me something new.

Scene 06 — Industry & Anxiety
Q

What's the hardest part of being a film student right now?

CL

The industry feels like it's in this chaotic, uncertain place — streaming wars, the strikes, AI, all of it. There's a lot of anxiety about whether there's room for the kind of slow, personal cinema I care about. But then I remind myself: people have always said cinema is dying, and it keeps reinventing itself. I'd rather be part of that reinvention than opt out of it.

"People have always said cinema is dying, and it keeps reinventing itself."
Scene 07 — The Hope
Q

What do you hope people take away from your films?

CL

Just that feeling I described earlier. Film isn't just what you see on screen — it's the feeling that stays with you after the lights come back on. If someone walks away from one of my films and feels less alone, or sees something in themselves they hadn't named before, that's everything.

Scene 08 — What's Next
Q

Any projects coming up you're excited about?

CL

I'm in pre-production on my thesis film. It's set in a laundromat — again, nothing "happens," but I think it's the most ambitious thing I've written. And I'm applying to some summer programs. Fingers crossed.
